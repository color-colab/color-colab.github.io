ANCIENT POLYCHROMY

Ancient polychromy has been proven beyond doubt: ancient sculptures were often painted with a variety of pigments. The evidence is copious multifaceted—including pigments that have remained in statues’ surfaces. For centuries, however, many archeologists and museum curators have been scrubbing away these traces of color before presenting statues and architectural reliefs to the general public. Some scholars have called ancient polychromy the ‘best kept secret’ of western art history, fueled by material and scholarly omission, with critical consequences for the perception of what should be considered within the Western canon of art. Furthermore, the willful ignorance of classical polychromy has also affected the perception of who can own, or not, the cultural heritage of classical ancient sculpture.

We now have incredibly sophisticated technologies, including Augmented Reality (AR), which allows us to project new information on artifacts without harming their integrity. Digital reproductions— computer animations and the like—can offer advantages that physical ones do not. They can be revised as new information becomes available, and they can also show multiple possibilities of how an object might have looked. This project proposes the utilization of AR technology to create an interactive art installation around classical sculptures, initially in museums and later in public spaces. By giving participants new knowledge and a new perspective on such historically relevant artifacts, they would also be given a chance to re-conceive and re-discover a higher complexity to the cultural heritage around them.
